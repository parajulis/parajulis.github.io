## About

I am an applied economist working at the intersection of agricultural risk, producer decision-making, and geospatial data. My research draws on econometric methods, remote sensing, and behavioral frameworks to understand how producers respond to risk and how policies and programs can be better designed to support them — spanning questions from conservation practice adoption to index insurance to the psychological drivers of agricultural entrepreneurship.

My MS thesis at the University of Georgia, *"Economic and Risk Implications of Cover Crop Adoption in Georgia Row Crop Systems,"* examined the on-farm tradeoffs shaping cover crop adoption among Georgia row-crop producers, under the direction of Dr. Yangxuan Liu. This built on earlier graduate work at the Agriculture and Forestry University (AFU) in Nepal, where I applied the Theory of Planned Behavior and PLS-SEM methods to study entrepreneurial intention among undergraduate agriculture students — my first exposure to modeling the behavioral side of agricultural decisions. More recently, I've extended this work geospatially, building dashboards linking NDVI and rainfall-index data to risk and insurance outcomes in Georgia cotton systems. I will begin a PhD in Agricultural and Applied Economics at UGA in Fall 2026, continuing across these threads with a stronger geospatial and remote-sensing emphasis.

### M.S. Thesis

#### Economic and Risk Implications of Cover Crop Adoption in Georgia Row Crop Systems

*Advisor: Dr. Yangxuan Liu*

<details>
<summary><b>Abstract</b></summary>
<p>Despite government support and farmer awareness, adoption of conservation practices remains low. Adoption is largely influenced by perceived economic performance. This study uses 2018–2023 field trial data from corn, peanut, and cotton production systems in annual rotation. Four treatment groups were rye, rye plus crimson clover, a mixed cover crop blend, and no cover crop (control). The objectives of this study were to analyze profitability, identify risk-efficient practices, and quantify utility-weighted risk premiums by employing a simulation-based approach, budgeting techniques, and the Stochastic Efficiency with Respect to a Function (SERF) framework. The control treatment revealed both the highest yield and net returns across all treatments. While the mixed cover crop was preferred across all risk-aversion levels for corn, the control treatment remained the most preferred option for peanut and cotton production. Risk premia ranged from $297.88 to $304.87 per ha for peanut, and $278.38 to $292.03 per ha for cotton.</p>
</details>

### Background

Before coming to UGA, I was affiliated with the Department of Agricultural Extension and Rural Sociology at AFU in Nepal, and worked as an agricultural officer. That experience — working directly with producers on extension and adoption questions — is a large part of what pulled me toward the quantitative side of agricultural economics: I wanted better tools for understanding *why* producers make the decisions they do, and for evaluating whether the programs meant to support them actually work.
